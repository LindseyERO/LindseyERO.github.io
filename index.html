Background

A little about me

I am an Associate Professor of English and Medical Humanities at Lakewood University, where I have taught since 2012. My work sits at the intersection of literary studies, narrative medicine, and the history of health care — asking what it means to tell a story about suffering, and who gets to tell it.

Before joining Lakewood, I completed my PhD at the University of Michigan and held a postdoctoral fellowship at the NYU School of Medicine. I grew up in rural Appalachia, which deeply informs my interest in place, community, and access to care.

Outside the classroom, I can usually be found in the garden, at a used bookshop, or attempting ambitious bread recipes with mixed success.

Scholarly Interests

Areas of Research

Narrative Medicine

Stories of Illness and Recovery

Examining how patients, caregivers, and clinicians construct meaning through narrative — and how those stories can both heal and harm.

19th–20th Century Literature

The Body in American Fiction

Tracing representations of chronic illness, disability, and contagion in American literary texts from the Civil War to the postwar period.

Health Humanities

Appalachian Health Narratives

An ongoing ethnographic and archival project documenting oral histories of health, land, and community in rural Appalachian communities.

Pedagogy

Empathy Education in Medical Training

Partnering with the School of Medicine to embed literary close-reading practices into clinical education, with a focus on underserved populations.
